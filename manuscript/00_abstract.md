# Abstract

Inclusive validation tests of collider observables can admit multiple nuisance explanations when analyses rely on reduced-observability data formats. This study investigates this identifiability problem using the missing transverse momentum (MET) distribution in Z→μ⁺μ⁻ events reconstructed from CMS Open Data. Within the CMS Collaboration, MET performance has been extensively characterized using full detector information and dedicated systematic variations. By contrast, external analyses using CMS Open Data are necessarily restricted to reduced-tier representations, such as NanoAOD, which expose MET primarily as a reconstructed end-product. Distinct nuisance mechanisms are shown to reproduce indistinguishable inclusive MET distributions, demonstrating that compression of high-dimensional collider events into reduced observables can induce non-identifiability in standard goodness-of-fit validation. The degeneracy demonstrated here does not imply that the underlying nuisance mechanisms are indistinguishable in the full event space; rather, it arises because standard validation tests operate on reduced observables that are not sufficient statistics for the underlying mechanisms.

The goal is not to improve MET reconstruction or reproduce collaboration-grade systematics, but to assess whether distinct, physically plausible nuisance explanations can be discriminated using only public-tier observables. Two low-dimensional phenomenological nuisance model families are constructed, correlated respectively with event topology (jet multiplicity reweighting) and recoil response (pT(Z)-correlated MET scale), and each is tuned to reproduce the observed MET distribution. A controlled injection framework is used to evaluate whether these incompatible explanations can be tuned to reproduce the same target MET distribution and whether the inclusive projection is sufficient to discriminate between them. Stratified diagnostics conditioned on pT(Z) and jet multiplicity are further examined for their ability to restore discrimination.

The inclusive MET projection is found to provide insufficient discrimination across a range of physically motivated perturbations. A topology-mixture nuisance family reproduces the inclusive distribution with χ²/dof = 3.13/11 (p = 0.989), while a detector-level perturbation derived from CMS unclustered-energy systematic variations lies near the boundary of inclusive discriminability (χ² = 18.28, p = 0.075). Stratified projections conditioned on pT(Z) and jet multiplicity recover discrimination, yielding Δχ² = 1,432 in the highest pT(Z) stratum and Δχ² = 3,415 in the ≥2-jet category. These results do not challenge collaboration MET performance studies, but instead quantify an inference boundary relevant to external analyses using open data. The strength of the stratified discrepancy depends on the nuisance mechanism, reflecting which latent assumptions of the model are violated. These results reflect a general consequence of information compression in collider data tiers: reducing observable dimensionality to high-level reconstructed quantities removes the latent structure required to distinguish mechanistically distinct explanations at the inclusive level. The analysis demonstrates that conditional stratification provides a simple diagnostic for detecting nuisance-model degeneracies and mapping the identifiability boundary of compressed collider data representations.
